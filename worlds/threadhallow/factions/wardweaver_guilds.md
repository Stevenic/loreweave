# The Wardweaver Guilds

*Urban protectors, political power-brokers, and gatekeepers of Threadcraft education.*

---

## Overview

Wardweavers are the establishment. Where Threadwalkers patrol the wilds, Wardweavers maintain the wards that protect settlements — permanent defensive patterns woven into a town's boundaries, buildings, and infrastructure.

They are respected, well-funded, and politically connected. In major cities, the Wardweaver Guild is as important as the city guard. In towns, the local Wardweaver is second only to the mayor in practical authority. In villages, a Wardweaver's presence means safety — their absence means fear.

**Headquarters:** Each major city has its own Guild chapter. The **Grand Loom** in the city of **Stitchford** serves as the coordinating body, though guild chapters jealously guard their autonomy.

**Size:** ~5,000 active Wardweavers. More numerous than Threadwalkers but concentrated in settled areas.

**Reputation:** Trusted by city-dwellers, resented by some villages (guilds charge for services), and viewed as politically compromised by the Threadwalkers and Rememberers.

## Structure

### Ranks

| Rank | Role | Typical Location |
|------|------|------------------|
| **Apprentice** | Student, 3–5 year training period | Guild chapter |
| **Stitchwright** | Journeyman. Maintains existing wards | Towns, assigned by guild |
| **Wardkeeper** | Senior. Designs and installs new wards | Cities, major towns |
| **Knotmaster/Knotmother** | Guild chapter leader | One per major city |
| **Grand Knotmother** | Coordinator of all chapters | Stitchford |

### Economics

Wardweaving is a profession, not a calling. Wardweavers are paid — by cities through taxes, by towns through tithe, and by villages through whatever they can offer (often food, lodging, and a stipend). The guilds control training and certification. Practicing Threadcraft without guild membership is technically illegal in most cities, though enforcement varies.

This creates a tension: guild Wardweavers are skilled but expensive. Villages that can't afford one rely on folk traditions — which the guilds dismiss as ineffective. (The guilds are wrong about this. Folk traditions are often older and tap into deeper patterns, but they lack precision.)

## Political Dynamics

### The Guild's Blind Spot

The Wardweaver Guilds are *good at what they do*. Their wards work. Their techniques are refined over centuries. This success has bred complacency:

- They believe the Thinning is a temporary fluctuation, not an existential threat
- They resist the idea that their understanding of the weave is incomplete
- They are threatened by anyone who practices Threadcraft outside guild structures (Threadwalkers, Common Folk, the Hollow Court)
- They have a financial incentive to maintain the status quo — if the Thinning is manageable with more Wardweavers, the answer is always "fund the guilds"

### Internal Factions

- **Traditionalists** — "The wards have held for centuries. The system works. Increase funding, train more Wardweavers." Led by Knotmother Sable.
- **Reformists** — "We need to study the Thinning, not just ward against it. Collaborate with the Threadwalkers." A minority, led quietly by Wardkeeper **Elm Bindfast** in Loomhearth.
- **Isolationists** — "Each chapter should focus on its own city. We can't save the wilds." A growing voice in wealthy, well-warded cities.

## Key NPCs

### Knotmother Sable

**Role:** Leader of the Loomhearth Wardweaver Guild chapter. One of the most powerful Wardweavers alive.

**Personality:** Sharp, elegant, politically masterful. She genuinely believes the guilds are the world's best defense. She's not corrupt — she's *wrong*, and her certainty makes her dangerous. She will oppose the player's investigations in Act II, not out of malice but out of institutional reflex.

**Arc:** If the player brings evidence from the Weavers' Cradle (Act III), Sable's response determines a political fork. She can accept the new truth and become an ally (requiring significant persuasion and evidence), or she can reject it and become an obstacle (forcing the player to work around her).

### Wardkeeper Elm Bindfast

**Role:** Mid-rank Wardweaver in Loomhearth. Guild reformist.

**Personality:** Quiet, observant, frustrated. He's noticed anomalies in the ward patterns that don't match the guild's models. He's been documenting them privately because questioning guild doctrine openly is career suicide.

**Arc:** Natural ally for the player in Loomhearth. Can be recruited to the eventual Speaker order (Harmony path) or to help design Ward Engines (Release path).

## Wardweaving Traditions

- **The Annual Inspection** — Every ward in a guild's territory is inspected yearly. The inspection is both maintenance and political event — communities celebrate the Wardweaver's visit.
- **Guild-names** — Like Threadwalkers, senior Wardweavers take professional names: Knotmother Sable, Stitchwright Elm, Wardkeeper Ashe.
- **The Loom Test** — Apprentice graduation exam. The candidate must weave a ward from scratch under guild observation. Failure means another year of training. Cheating means expulsion.
- **The Mender's Oath** — "I weave so that others may dwell." Sworn at graduation. Some older Wardweavers note bitterly that the oath says "others," not "paying clients."
