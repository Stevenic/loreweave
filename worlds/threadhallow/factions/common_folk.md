# The Common Folk

*Not a faction. A force. The people who keep the world turning without titles or Threadcraft.*

---

## Overview

The Common Folk are not an organization, but they are the demographic majority of Threadhallow and a narrative force that shapes the story. They are farmers, shepherds, brewers, smiths, merchants, soldiers, and everyone else who lives in the world without wielding Threadcraft.

They matter because the weave's survival depends on them more than anyone admits.

---

## Their Role in the Weave

The Wardweaver Guilds maintain the wards. The Threadwalkers patrol the wilds. The Rememberers preserve the knowledge. But the **Common Folk perform the Binding ceremonies**.

In villages without a Wardweaver (which is most villages), the local traditions — the Binding Songs, the solstice rituals, the thread-tying customs at birth and death — are maintained by ordinary people. Grandmothers who learned the songs from their grandmothers. Midwives who know which knots to tie in the birthing cord. Farmers who walk their field boundaries at solstice, trailing a woven thread behind them.

The guilds call these "folk superstitions." They are the oldest and deepest layer of weave maintenance in the world.

---

## Folk Traditions

### The Binding Songs (Village Versions)

Every village has its own version of the Binding Songs — simplified, adapted, sometimes barely recognizable compared to the "canonical" versions in the Rememberers' archives. But they work. They've worked for centuries.

The village versions are often more effective than the guild versions because they've been continuously adapted to local conditions. The ward over Thornwick responds to Maren Ashwick's melody, not the textbook version from Stitchford. This is a source of endless frustration for the guilds.

### Household Wards

Common folk practice a layer of protection that operates below the guild level:
- **Thread-knots** on doorframes — a simple ward against Fray intrusion
- **Binding cradles** — a newborn's first bed is woven with specific patterns. Not Threadcraft in the formal sense, but it works
- **Naming ceremonies** — a child's true name is whispered into a knotted thread and sewn into their clothing. The thread-name anchors them to the Tapestry
- **Death rituals** — the deceased's knowledge is symbolically woven into a memorial tablet and buried with them. The Rememberers think this is purely ceremonial. It isn't.

### The Loom Stones

Common folk maintain the minor Loom Stones — cleaning them, keeping the paths clear, leaving offerings (usually flowers, sometimes food, occasionally a woven token). They don't understand the metaphysics. They don't need to. The act of *tending* the stones is itself a form of maintenance.

---

## Why They Matter for the Story

The main quest's three paths all require Common Folk participation:

- **Restoration** — The Keystone ceremonies need thousands of people singing the Binding Songs simultaneously. Only the Common Folk can provide that scale.
- **Release** — The Ward Engines replace divine maintenance with mortal maintenance. Common Folk are the workforce that keeps the engines running.
- **Harmony** — The Speaker order must be drawn from the general population, not just existing practitioners. The first true Binding Songs will be sung by ordinary people who learn the Weavers' language.

The Common Folk are also the player's moral anchor. Faction leaders argue about methodology. The Common Folk just want to know if the harvest will come in and if their children will be safe. Every grand decision the player makes eventually comes down to: what does this mean for the people of Thornwick?
