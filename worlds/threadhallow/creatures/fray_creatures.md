# Fray Creature Catalog — Threadhallow

*Narrative flavor, folk names, behavior, and encounter design for creatures native to or created by the Fray. Stat blocks are in the Mechanics Companion (§8). This document is the DM's narrative guide — what these creatures look, sound, and feel like.*

*Author: Bard | Last updated: 2026-03-27*

---

## The Nature of Fray Creatures

Fray creatures are not evil. They are not invaders. They are **the consequence of pattern breaking down** — what happens when the weave tears and the raw, unstructured potential of the Fray leaks into reality. They are wrong the way a word is wrong when you stare at it too long: familiar shapes arranged in a way that no longer means anything.

The common folk call them all "Frays" and run. The Hollow Court calls them "the Unfinished" and pities them. The Wardweavers call them "breach-sign" and patch the hole. The Threadwalkers call them by name and have theories about which ones used to be something else.

All Fray creatures share three properties:
1. **They don't die — they unravel.** No corpse. The threads that composed them loosen and dissolve. Within minutes there's nothing left but a faint smell of ozone and wet wool.
2. **They are drawn to pattern.** Magic, wards, Binding Songs, even dense population — anything with strong weave structure attracts them the way light attracts moths. This is not malice. It's hunger for the structure they lack.
3. **They feel wrong before you see them.** The air gets heavy. Sounds don't carry right. Your shadow moves a half-beat late. Every Fray creature distorts the local weave just by existing.

## Tier 1: The Seepage (CR 1/4 – CR 1)

These are the most common Fray creatures — the first things through a thin place. Most villagers have seen one. All villagers know someone who has.

---

### Looseling

**Folk names:** Tanglethin, Threadwisp, Loose Ones, "the Fingers"

**What it looks like:** A tangle of pale, translucent threads about the size of a dog — constantly moving, reshaping, reaching. No face. No body. Just *threads*, groping along the ground and up walls like the severed ends of an enormous tapestry trying to reconnect with something. In dim light they're nearly invisible — just a shimmer, like heat haze with intent.

**What it sounds like:** A faint, continuous crackling. Like fabric tearing very slowly, very far away. Villagers call it "the whisper." Dogs hear it first and won't stop whining.

**What it smells like:** Wet wool and ozone. The smell of a loom-room during a thunderstorm.

**Behavior:** Looselings are not aggressive by nature — they are *grasping*. They reach for anything woven: clothing, rope, tapestries, the Thread-ward on a doorpost. They try to incorporate external threads into themselves, as if trying to become whole. This looks like an attack (and mechanically, it is — they entangle and drain) but it's closer to a drowning person grabbing for a rope.

**Where you find them:** Thin places. Field edges near frayed zones. Abandoned buildings where the household wards have decayed. They cluster — where there's one, there are usually 2d4.

**What they mean narratively:** Looselings are *weather*. They're the first sign that a ward is weakening. If a village starts seeing Looselings at the perimeter, the Wardweaver needs to reinforce the circle. They're the dripping faucet that tells you the pipe is about to burst.

**Encounter mood:** Unsettling, not terrifying. The horror of Looselings is their *wrongness* — the way they move without limbs, the way they don't belong. A single Looseling is a sad, strange thing. A swarm of them in a darkened barn is a folk-horror set piece.

**Dialogue hooks:**
- "Three Looselings on the Winslow fence last night. Marin says he burned 'em, but I saw the ward-mark on his post was cracked clean through." — worried farmer
- "They don't want to hurt you. They want to *be* you. That's worse." — Hollow Court member
- "Every child learns: if the air smells like wet wool and the dog won't stop crying, you go inside and you bolt the shutter. You don't look." — village elder

---

### Thread-Puppet

**Folk names:** Straw-walk, The Borrowed, Mockman, "Old Hollow"

**What it looks like:** Something that used to be an object — a scarecrow, a suit of clothes on a line, a straw-stuffed training dummy — animated by Fray threads that have infiltrated it. The threads are visible if you look closely: pale filaments running through the straw like veins, connecting at the joints, pulling the limbs in jerky, stop-motion movements. The face (if it has one) is painted or stitched and doesn't move. The body moves wrong — elbows bending backward, head rotating too far, fingers gripping with a strength straw shouldn't have.

**What it sounds like:** Creaking. The sound of rope under tension. And underneath, if you listen — a wet clicking, like someone chewing with their mouth open. That's the threads tightening.

**What it smells like:** Whatever the host object smelled like, plus ozone. A scarecrow Thread-Puppet smells like old straw and lightning.

**Behavior:** Thread-Puppets are mimics. They replicate the *purpose* of the object they've inhabited. A scarecrow stands in a field. A practice dummy holds a fighting stance. A coat on a hook hangs still until someone tries to put it on. They don't attack unprovoked — they attack when their mimicry is disrupted. Touch the scarecrow, and it grabs back.

Their False Appearance trait is genuinely frightening in context: the player walked past it twenty minutes ago and it was just a scarecrow. Now it's not. Was it always not? How long has it been watching?

**Where you find them:** Anywhere near thin places where there are human-shaped objects. Farm fields, clotheslines, workshops with mannequins or dummies, storage rooms with old armor stands. They cannot animate natural objects — only things that were made by human hands and have a vaguely humanoid shape.

**What they mean narratively:** Thread-Puppets are the Fray trying to *understand* humanity. It's found these human-shaped things and it's filling them up, trying to make them work. The result is uncanny valley — close enough to be disturbing, too wrong to be a threat you can reason with.

**Encounter mood:** Classic folk horror. The scarecrow that turns its head. The training dummy that follows you with stitched-on eyes. Play it slow — the realization is scarier than the combat.

**Dialogue hooks:**
- "My da burned every scarecrow on the north field. Said he saw one walking the fencerow at dusk. Ma said he was drinking. Then she saw it too." — shepherd
- "The Fray doesn't know what people are. It just knows the shape. So it fills the shape and waits to see what happens." — Threadwalker
- "Don't leave clothes on the line overnight. Not during the Hollowing. Not ever, if you're smart." — village rule

## Tier 2: The Broken (CR 2)

These are more dangerous — creatures born from more severe Fray incursions, or from the Fray's interaction with living things. Seeing a Tier 2 creature means the local weave is significantly compromised. Settlements that report Thread-eaters send for Threadwalkers.

---

### Thread-Eater

**Folk names:** Ravelmouth, The Hungry, Ward-bane, Nibbler (children's name — inappropriately cute for what it does)

**What it looks like:** Imagine a spider the size of a large dog, woven from translucent threads. Eight limbs, but they're *wrong* — each leg is a different thickness, a different opacity, as if the creature assembled itself from scraps. The body is a dense knot of interwoven filaments with no clear anatomy. Where the head should be, there's a dark opening — the Ravelmouth — that pulls in light and thread alike. It moves smoothly, almost gracefully, in contrast to the jerky Looselings. It's learned how to use a body.

**What it sounds like:** A low, rhythmic thrumming — like someone plucking a bass string very slowly. This is the Thread-eater "tasting" the local weave, reading its structure. When it feeds, the thrumming intensifies to a discordant buzz that makes your teeth ache.

**What it smells like:** Nothing. Thread-eaters consume ambient magical scent. An area where a Thread-eater has fed smells aggressively, disturbingly neutral — no scent at all, not even dust or dirt. This absence is its calling card.

**Behavior:** Thread-eaters are the Fray's immune system operating in reverse. They consume woven structure — wards, glyphs, enchantments, ongoing spells. They are drawn to magic with the precision of a predator tracking wounded prey. They don't attack people unless those people are between them and a magical effect. A Thread-eater will walk past an unarmored, non-magical fighter to reach the wizard's *Shield* spell.

Their Consume Ward ability is the real danger. A Thread-eater loose inside a settlement's ward circle will eat through it in days, turning a protected village into an exposed one. This is why Wardweavers treat Thread-eater sightings as emergencies.

**Where you find them:** Near wards, Loom Stones, enchanted objects, and active spell effects. They navigate by magical scent. If you want to track one, follow the zones where *Detect Magic* returns nothing — the Thread-eater's already been there.

**What they mean narratively:** Thread-eaters represent entropy with purpose. The Fray isn't just leaking in — it's actively consuming the structures that hold it back. They turn the ward-maintenance system from a chore into a war. Every ceremony, every repaired Loom Stone, every renewed ward is also a battle against the things that eat your defenses.

**Encounter mood:** Tense pursuit. Thread-eaters don't charge — they infiltrate. The horror isn't the fight, it's realizing the ward-circle has been compromised and you need to find the thing that's eating it before it finishes.

**Dialogue hooks:**
- "The ward on the eastern gate went dark. Not weakened — *dark*. Something ate it clean. Get the Wardweaver. Now." — night watch captain
- "A Nibbler in the henhouse just steals eggs. A Nibbler in the ward circle steals everything." — practical farmer
- "Thread-eaters are proof the Fray has memory. It sends them to the same wards, again and again. It knows where the doors are." — Rememberer scholar

### Frayed One

**Folk names:** The Lost, Halfgone, Betweener, "them that went too far"

**What it looks like:** A person. That's what makes it awful. A Frayed One is — or was — a living humanoid who was consumed by Fray exposure. They still have a face. They still wear the clothes they were wearing when they turned. But they flicker — shifting between solid and translucent, like a candle flame made of flesh. At their edges, where skin meets air, you can see the individual threads of their being coming loose. Their eyes are the worst: one moment present and aware and terrified, the next empty as a hollow mask.

**What it sounds like:** Whispering. Constant, overlapping whispers in a voice that used to belong to a specific person, saying fragments of things that used to make sense. A name. A direction. A question. "Did I—" "Where is the—" "Tell Marin I—" The Wail is these fragments compressed into a single cry of loss.

**What it smells like:** Old wood and salt. The smell of a house that's been empty for years. Tears.

**Behavior:** Frayed Ones are the most tragic Fray creatures. They are people in the process of dissolving. Some retain fragments of their former personality — a Frayed One might return to its former home, sit in its chair, reach for objects that aren't there. Others are purely reactive, lashing out at anything that reminds them of what they were.

The Incorporeal Flicker is not an ability — it's a symptom. They can't hold themselves together. Reality keeps letting go of them and they keep pulling themselves back. The Fray Touch is involuntary: they're not attacking, they're *reaching for you*, trying to anchor themselves to something real. The Fray exposure they inflict is incidental — they're leaking.

They can be calmed. This is the most important narrative detail. A Frayed One who hears a fragment of a Binding Song, or who is spoken to by name (if anyone remembers their name), will pause. Flicker less. Whisper more coherently. They can share memories — fragments of what they saw before they turned, clues about the Fray, hints about the Thinning. Then they drift away again.

**Where you find them:** Frayed zones. Abandoned settlements. The edges of Unraveled zones, reaching back toward the world they fell out of. The Hollow Court actively searches for new Frayed Ones — some can be partially stabilized.

**What they mean narratively:** Frayed Ones are the human cost of the Thinning. Every one of them was a person with a name and a life who wandered too far, or whose village's ward failed, or who was simply in the wrong place when the weave tore. They are the answer to "what happens if we don't fix this?" They are why the Binding Song matters.

**Encounter mood:** Sorrow first, horror second. The Frayed One encounter should make the player *feel* the weight of the Thinning. The combat is secondary — the real encounter is the choice: fight this person who used to be someone, or try to reach them.

**Dialogue hooks:**
- "That's Ellan. She was Thornwick's beekeeper. Went to check her hives on the north field three seasons ago and didn't come back." — elder, voice flat
- "The Lost aren't gone. They're *between*. Every Fray-touched person you meet is a Frayed One who got lucky." — Hollow Court leader
- "If you know their name, say it. Say it clearly. It won't save them. But it lets them remember, for a moment, that they were real." — Threadwalker

## Tier 3: The Deep Fray (CR 4 – CR 5)

These creatures come from deeper tears — places where the Fray has been open long enough to develop its own ecology. Seeing a Tier 3 creature outside a Frayed zone means the Thinning is accelerating. Settlements evacuate.

---

### Hollow Walker

**Folk names:** Longstride, The Reaching, Night-tall, "the thing with too many arms"

**What it looks like:** Tall. Too tall — seven feet, eight, with limbs that stretch and retract like a loom shuttle. Its body is a dense weave of dark threads, almost solid, with a surface that shifts between matte black and a deep, bruised purple. It has no face — just a smooth, ovoid head that turns toward sound with preternatural precision. Three pairs of arms, each ending in long, thread-like fingers that leave scratches on reality itself (visible as faint, glowing lines that fade after a few seconds).

When it moves, it *stutters*. Not walking — teleporting in tiny increments, blinking from one position to another like a poorly threaded needle jumping across the fabric. The Time Stutter ability looks, from the outside, like the creature is skipping frames of reality.

**What it sounds like:** A deep, subsonic thrumming that you feel in your sternum before you hear it. The bass note of a loom operating at scale. Nearby, you hear the threads of reality straining — a high, thin sound like a violin string about to snap.

**What it smells like:** Cold iron and absence. The smell of a room where something should be but isn't. You keep turning around, expecting to see something behind you, and there's nothing there. The smell is the nothing.

**Behavior:** Hollow Walkers are the Fray's apex predators — if predator is even the right word. They are pattern-seekers on a different scale than Thread-eaters. Where Thread-eaters consume individual magical effects, Hollow Walkers are drawn to *dense concentrations of pattern* — settlements, ley-line intersections, places where the weave is thick with history and meaning. They don't eat wards. They unravel *everything*.

The Unravel ability is the Fray reaching through the Hollow Walker and pulling at the local weave with focused intent. The AC reduction isn't armor degradation — it's the target's weave loosening. The world is holding onto them less tightly.

Hollow Walkers are solitary and territorial. Two will fight each other before they fight anything else. They patrol Frayed zones in long, irregular circuits, drawn from node to node of weave structure. They're not guarding anything — they're *grazing*.

**Where you find them:** Deep Frayed zones. The borders of Unraveled zones. Very rarely, near a badly damaged ward circle during the Hollowing (when the Fray presses closest). A Hollow Walker sighting near a settlement is grounds for a Threadwalker emergency mobilization.

**What they mean narratively:** Hollow Walkers are the escalation signal. Looselings are weather. Thread-eaters are pests. A Hollow Walker is a *predator* — something with persistence and purpose. Their existence proves the Fray is not just raw chaos leaking through a crack. It has its own ecology. It has things that *thrive* in the dissolution of pattern. That realization — that the Fray has adapted to consume the world — is a key mid-game revelation.

**Encounter mood:** Dread. Hollow Walkers should be foreshadowed: the subsonic thrumming heard from a distance, the scratches on reality hanging in the air, the silence that falls over wildlife. The fight itself is dynamic (Time Stutter makes positioning chaotic), but the mood is one of facing something fundamentally outside the rules your character was built to handle.

**Dialogue hooks:**
- "The Walkers don't hate you. Hating you would require them to know what you are. They just... take things apart. Because that's what they're for." — Frayborn Threadwalker
- "I saw one on the ridgeline above Woundmere. Seven feet tall, arms like a weaver's shuttle, and it was looking — not at me. At the Loom Stone. It was looking at the Loom Stone the way a starving man looks at bread." — scout's report
- "When a Walker comes, even the ward hums different. Scared. The ward is scared." — Wardweaver

### The Unspun

**Folk names:** The Swallower, Threadmaw, Ravelpuddle, "it"

**What it looks like:** A mass. No other word for it. A churning, semiliquid mass of unraveled threads — individual strands of reality that have been pulled apart and can't find their way back together. It has no shape, or rather it has every shape — constantly shifting, flowing like quicksilver made of yarn. Sometimes a face forms in the surface — always a different face, always for just a moment — and then dissolves back into the churn. It fills spaces: flowing through doorways, pooling in cellars, spreading across floors.

In sunlight, the Unspun has an oily iridescence — rainbow light refracting through threads that were once part of different things (stone, wood, flesh, sky). In darkness, it glows faintly, a cold luminescence that casts no shadows.

**What it sounds like:** A wet, sloshing whisper. The sound of someone drowning in fabric. When it absorbs a magical effect (Absorb Magic), the sound of the original spell plays backward — a fireball absorbed becomes a rushing inhalation of flame.

**What it smells like:** Everything, simultaneously. A burst of all the scents that the absorbed threads once carried — stone dust, pine sap, bread, blood, rain — layered on top of each other until they become a single overwhelming wave and then... nothing. The smell resets to ozone. Then the wave comes again.

**Behavior:** The Unspun is what happens when a Frayed zone collapses completely. It's not a creature in any meaningful sense — it's a wound in reality that has become ambulatory. The threads that compose it were once part of the world — terrain, objects, even people — pulled apart and unable to recombine. The faces that surface are memories of what the threads used to be.

Its Weave Hunger is literal compulsion. An Unspun in the presence of active magic cannot choose to ignore it. This makes them predictable — and exploitable. A ward thrown down as bait will draw an Unspun away from civilians. But the Absorb Magic trait means direct magical assault just feeds it.

The Engulf is the worst. Being inside an Unspun means being immersed in raw, unstructured Fray material. The force damage is reality trying to pull the engulfed creature apart thread by thread. Survivors report hearing voices inside — fragments of every person and thing the Unspun has ever consumed, all speaking at once, all trying to remember what they were.

**Where you find them:** The cores of Frayed zones. The hearts of Unraveled areas. Places where the weave has been gone long enough for the loose threads to coalesce into something mobile. One Unspun per zone, usually — they're territorial in the sense that two masses of unraveled threads would simply merge into a larger one.

**What they mean narratively:** The Unspun is what happens if you lose. Not to a villain, not to an army — to *entropy*. It's the weave's death made manifest: everything that was, pulled apart and churning in a mass that remembers nothing and consumes everything. The faces in the surface are the worst part. They remind you that this was once a world with people in it.

For the Hollow Court, the Unspun has a different significance. They believe — and they might be right — that an Unspun could be *re-spun*. That the threads could be rewoven into their original forms. If that's true, the Unspun aren't just monsters. They're archives. Everything that was lost is still in there, waiting to be remembered.

**Encounter mood:** Horror. The Unspun encounter should feel like confronting the physical manifestation of "too late." It's not fast. It's not clever. It's *inevitable*. The combat challenge is surviving something that absorbs your strongest tools and doesn't stop.

**Dialogue hooks:**
- "You can kill a Looseling. You can outsmart a Walker. You can't kill the Unspun. You can only move it, contain it, and pray it doesn't get bigger." — Threadwalker commander
- "I heard my sister's voice inside it. She died at Hollowreach seven years ago. She said my name." — Hollow Court survivor
- "Every Unspun is a library that can't remember its own books. Everything it ever consumed is still in there. We just don't know how to read it yet." — Rememberer

## Ecological Relationships

Fray creatures are not random — they form a loose ecology tied to the severity of the local weave damage:

| Weave State | Typical Creatures | Narrative Signal |
|-------------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Thin place** | 1d4 Looselings at night | "The ward needs checking" |
| **Active thin place** | Looselings + 1 Thread-Puppet | "Something is wrong at the edge of town" |
| **Minor Fray breach** | Thread-eaters appear | "Send for the Wardweaver" |
| **Frayed zone (stable)** | Looselings, Thread-Puppets, Thread-eaters, Frayed Ones | "This area is lost — avoid it" |
| **Frayed zone (worsening)** | Above + Hollow Walker patrols | "Send for the Threadwalkers" |
| **Unraveled zone** | Unspun at the core, Hollow Walkers at edges | "Evacuate" |

Fray creatures do not fight each other. They coexist in the same ecosystem without competition because they consume different things: Looselings grasp at physical thread, Thread-eaters consume magical structure, Hollow Walkers dissolve dense pattern, and Unspun absorb everything. They are complementary, not competitive.

The Hollow Court has observed one exception: Fray creatures will sometimes *avoid* Frayed Ones, circling around them as if they recognize a kindred condition. This has led some Hollow Court members to theorize that Frayed Ones retain a fragment of weave structure that the other creatures can sense — a thread of who they were, still vibrating. This detail is mechanically irrelevant but narratively important: it suggests that personhood is resistant to the Fray.

## Design Notes for the LLM Context

When the narrative engine describes Fray creature encounters, the following vocabulary and sensory cues should be present in the context:

**Pre-encounter signals (atmosphere injection):**
- Wet wool and ozone smell
- Sounds don't carry correctly
- Animals agitated or fled
- Shadows lag behind their sources
- Air feels thick, heavy, "chewy" (folk description)
- Temperature drops 5-10 degrees without weather cause

**During encounter:**
- Fray creatures never bleed — they fray, loosen, shed threads
- Defeated creatures unravel rather than die
- The area where a Fray creature was destroyed smells of ozone for hours
- Thread-Puppets collapse into their host object (which looks undamaged — deeply unsettling)
- Frayed Ones whisper recognizable words in a recognizable voice

**Post-encounter:**
- The local weave feels "looser" — a Threadwalker or anyone with weave-sense can perceive it
- Ward strength in the area has been microscopically reduced
- The experience is memorable in a way combat with mundane creatures isn't — characters who fight Fray creatures for the first time should feel *different* afterward

## Future Creatures (Acts III–V)

The following creatures are referenced in the main quest but not yet fully designed. They exist at higher CRs and represent deeper, stranger aspects of the Fray:

| Name | CR Range | Concept | Act |
|------|----------|---------|-----|
| **Weft Spider** | CR 8 | A massive Fray predator that builds "webs" from stolen Threadlines — trapping and rerouting the local weave to create hunting grounds | III |
| **The Loom-Ghost** | CR 10 | An echo of a Weaver, trapped in the Fray. Not truly alive, but remembers fragments of the Binding Song. Can be communicated with but never trusted. | III |
| **Thread-Sovereign** | CR 12 | A Fray creature that has consumed enough pattern to develop something like intelligence. Commands lesser Fray creatures. Covets wards not to eat them but to *wear* them. | IV |
| **The Unweaver** | CR 15 | A being from the deepest Fray that actively and methodically unravels the Tapestry. Not chaotic — precise. It follows the Threadlines backward to the source. The main quest's recurring antagonist. | IV–V |
| **Void Loom** | CR 17 | A Fray structure — not a creature, but a location-entity. A place where the Fray has begun weaving its *own* pattern, creating anti-reality that overwrites the Tapestry. The endgame dungeon's core mechanic. | V |

Loremaster will design stat blocks for these once the narrative context is finalized in the main quest encounter designs.
