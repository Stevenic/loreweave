# Act II Cast — The Threadwalker's Path

*The player leaves home. These are the people who show them what the world actually looks like.*

---

## Farweft — Threadwalker of the New Stitch

**Age:** 34. **Appearance:** Lean, weather-hardened, moves like someone who sleeps light and walks far. Close-cropped dark hair, practical. A thin scar across her left palm from a Fray burn she won't explain. Wears a Threadwalker's long coat — patched, repatched, and patched again. Carries a walking staff carved with trail signs from every Walk she's completed.

**Personality:** Farweft is intensity held on a leash. She transferred from the Mending Path to the New Stitch after her patrol partner Hest was killed — or so she believes — by a Fray manifestation that old techniques couldn't counter. She channels her grief into momentum. She doesn't rest well. She doesn't small-talk well. She walks.

Underneath the driven exterior is someone who genuinely loves the weave. She'll stop mid-journey to show the player how a Threadline catches light at dusk, or crouch by a stream to point out where the water follows a pattern older than the riverbed. She teaches without meaning to.

She's angry at the Mending Path for not adapting. She's frustrated with the New Stitch for not being serious enough. She respects the player because they *did something* — Thornwick proved that action matters more than tradition or theory.

**Voice:** Clipped, precise, warms unexpectedly when she's talking about the weave. Military cadence — years of patrol reports. Uses trail metaphors. "The path doesn't care what you expected. It is what it is. Walk it or don't."

**Role in story:** The player's guide out of the village world and into the wider Threadhallow landscape. She's the Act II primary companion by default. She knows the wilds, reads Threadlines like a tracker reads spoor, and has strong opinions about every faction the player meets. She's the lens through which the player first sees the political tensions.

**Arc:** Farweft begins certain the old ways failed Hest. Over the course of Act II–III, she discovers evidence that Hest didn't die — he was pulled into a sealed thin place. Finding him (partially Fray-touched, confused, alive) forces her to reconcile: the old ways didn't kill him, and the new ways didn't save him. The answer is between. She bridges the Mending Path and New Stitch orders, becoming the first walker genuinely fluent in both.

**Secret:** Farweft already uses one Mending Path technique she's never admitted to: the Silence Toast. She performs it privately, every meal, pouring a drop of water on bare earth for the Weavers. She left the order. She didn't leave the faith.

## Wren Looseweft — Blacksmith of the Hollow Court

**Age:** 40. **Appearance:** Broad-shouldered, calloused hands, forge-scarred forearms. Her eyes catch light wrong — a pale refraction at certain angles, the only visible sign of her Fray-touch. She keeps her hair braided tight and functional. Wears a leather apron over everything, even when she isn't forging. Carries a hammer at her belt the way a soldier carries a sword.

**Personality:** Wren is the most grounded person the player will meet. Before the Fray changed her, she was a blacksmith. After the Fray changed her, she is still a blacksmith. The world treated her transformation as a tragedy. She treated it as a material property change — like learning that a new ore behaves differently under heat. Adjust the technique. Keep working.

She leads Hollowreach not because she wanted to, but because someone had to organize the survivors and she was already organizing the salvage runs. She governs the way she forges: firm pressure, consistent heat, no wasted motion.

She has no patience for self-pity (her own or others'), no interest in factional politics, and absolute loyalty to anyone who treats her people with dignity. She doesn't care which path the player chooses as long as the Fray-touched have a future in it.

**Voice:** Dry, direct, occasional dark humor. Metalworking metaphors. "You don't fix a crack in steel by pretending it isn't there. You either work around it or you reforge. Your call."

**Role in story:** The player's introduction to the Hollow Court and the idea that the Fray isn't simply destructive. Negotiating passage through Hollowreach is the Act II social challenge that mirrors Act I's Sorrel persuasion. Wren reveals that the Fray-touched can *feel* the Threadlines being pulled, and the direction leads to the Weavers' Cradle.

**Arc:** Wren discovers she can forge Fray-touched iron — "Thread-Iron" — that physically interacts with the weave. This opens mechanical (non-magical) ward maintenance, essential for the Release path's Ward Engines. She goes from surviving-in-the-margins to holding a key the whole world needs.

**Secret:** Wren's Fray-touch is deeper than she lets on. She doesn't just have the sensory shift — she has nascent pattern sight. She sees the forge-fire differently now: the flames follow the Threadlines. She can forge along the weave's grain. She hasn't told anyone because she doesn't want to be studied. She wants to be a blacksmith.

## Knotmother Sable — Guild Chapter Leader, Loomhearth

**Age:** 55. **Appearance:** Silver-streaked dark hair, always impeccably arranged. Robes of deep indigo with guild thread-sigils woven into the hems — each one a ward she designed personally. Carries herself like someone who has never been in a room she didn't own. Hands are uncalloused — she designs wards now, she doesn't weave them herself.

**Personality:** Sable is the most dangerous person in Loomhearth, and it's not because of her Threadcraft. It's because she's *right about almost everything* — and the one thing she's wrong about is the thing that matters most.

She's right that the guilds are the most effective weave-maintenance organization. She's right that untrained Threadcraft is dangerous. She's right that the Threadwalkers' field experiments have caused real damage. She's right that funding the guilds saves lives. She is *wrong* that the Thinning is manageable with existing methods. And her certainty — built on decades of competence — makes her the biggest institutional obstacle in the player's path.

She is not corrupt. She does not scheme for personal power. She genuinely believes she is protecting people. That's what makes her terrifying.

**Voice:** Measured, elegant, never raises her voice. Uses silence as punctuation. When she disagrees, she asks questions that reveal the weakness in your position. "An interesting observation. And what evidence, specifically, contradicts the seventeen documented cases in which standard guild protocols stabilized the affected zone?"

**Role in story:** The political antagonist of Act II — not a villain, but a wall. She controls the Loomhearth guild chapter, which means she controls funding, certification, and institutional support. The player can work around her, persuade her, or make an enemy of her. Each choice has consequences.

**Arc:** If the player brings evidence from the Weavers' Cradle (Act III), Sable faces a crisis. Her entire career is built on a model of the weave that turns out to be incomplete. She can accept this and become one of the player's most powerful allies — a guild leader who redirects institutional resources toward the real problem. Or she can reject it and double down, becoming an Act IV obstacle who actively opposes whichever path the player chooses.

**Secret:** Sable's private journals record anomalies in ward behavior that she's never published. Patterns that don't match guild models. Decay rates that accelerate without cause. She's *noticed* — she just hasn't allowed herself to draw the obvious conclusion, because the conclusion would invalidate everything she's built.

## Pathmaster Aldric Longstitch — Mending Path Leader

**Age:** 52. **Appearance:** Tall, weathered, scarred. A long diagonal mark from right temple to left jaw — a Fray burn from a patrol gone wrong thirty years ago. Wears his gray hair long and tied back. His Threadwalker coat is the oldest anyone has seen, repaired so many times it's more patch than original fabric. Walks with a slight hitch in his left hip but never slows.

**Personality:** Longstitch is the Threadwalker the other Threadwalkers tell stories about. He's walked every major Threadline in the northern region at least twice. He's fought Fray creatures that most people have only heard of. He's buried more patrol partners than he cares to count.

None of this has made him hard. It's made him *careful*. He speaks softly because he's been in enough situations where loud meant dead. He thinks before answering because hasty decisions got people killed. He trusts in the old techniques because he's seen them work a thousand times — but he's honest enough to admit they don't work every time.

He's the adult in the room at Loomhearth. Where Sable defends the institution and Verse champions discovery, Longstitch just wants to know: what works? What will keep people alive? He'll listen to anyone who has an answer, regardless of faction.

**Voice:** Quiet, deliberate, economical. Trail-worn wisdom. "I've seen the Mending Songs hold a thin place for forty years. I've also seen them fail in an hour. The difference isn't the song — it's whether you understand what you're singing to."

**Role in story:** The player's Threadwalker authority contact in Loomhearth. He sanctions the expedition to the Weavers' Cradle — reluctantly, because he knows the dangers, but honestly, because he knows the need. He's the counterweight to Sable's opposition.

**Arc:** Longstitch is not a path-chooser in Act IV — he's a facilitator. Whichever path the player takes, Longstitch is the one who convinces the Threadwalker orders to cooperate. His reputation and his scars buy credibility that no argument could.

**Secret:** Longstitch lost his sense of smell to a Fray encounter twenty years ago. He's mildly Fray-touched and has never told anyone. He can feel thin places before seeing them — a pressure behind his eyes, like weather changing. He considers this a professional advantage, not a condition. If the Hollow Court knew, they'd welcome him. He'd rather keep walking.

## Verse — Keeper of the Third Verse, Senior Rememberer

**Age:** 47. **Appearance:** Short, round-faced, wire-framed glasses perpetually sliding down her nose. Ink-stained fingers, always. Her robes have more pockets than a peddler's coat, each containing a different notebook, thread-spool, or reference text. Her hair is an optimistic brown that's losing its argument with gray.

**Personality:** Verse is what happens when genuine brilliance meets genuine warmth. She looks scattered — fumbling for the right notebook, trailing off mid-sentence to follow a thought, forgetting she was speaking to someone. She is not scattered. She is *processing* — her mind runs five conversations simultaneously and occasionally the wrong one reaches her mouth.

When she focuses, she's razor-sharp. She's the senior Rememberer because she's the best political mind in the Archive, not because she wanted the job. She negotiates guild funding with the patience of a saint and the memory of an elephant. Knotmother Sable has tried to outmaneuver her three times. She has failed three times.

Verse loves knowledge the way some people love their children — fiercely, protectively, and with occasional exasperation. She's the first person in Loomhearth who takes the player's findings seriously, because she's had the same suspicions for years and been too politically careful to publish them.

**Voice:** Rapid, warm, digressive, then suddenly crystalline when she hits the point. "That's — yes, see, this is exactly what I was telling Elm last autumn, the resonance patterns in the Third Verse don't match any known Binding Song structure, which means either the original was corrupted in transmission, which, I mean, it's been twelve hundred years, or — or the Third Verse isn't a Binding Song at all. It's a *reply*."

**Role in story:** The intellectual ally at Loomhearth. She provides access to the Archive's private collection (the texts the guild would rather suppress). Her unpublished theory — that Binding Songs are conversations, not rituals — is the conceptual bridge to Act III's revelation.

**Arc:** Verse becomes the architect of the Harmony path. She designs the Speaker training program and composes the framework for the first true Binding Song. On other paths, she's the researcher who ensures the player has the knowledge they need.

**Secret:** Verse once heard a Binding Song *answer*. During a solstice ceremony at the Archive, she sang the Third Verse and heard a harmonic that no one else could perceive — a lower tone, like the same melody played backward and slower. She's spent seven years trying to reproduce it. She hasn't told anyone because she can't prove it happened and she refuses to publish without evidence.

## Elm Bindfast — Wardweaver Reformist

**Age:** 38. **Appearance:** Medium build, neat brown beard, tired eyes. Dresses in standard guild robes but with the sleeves rolled up — a Wardweaver who still does fieldwork. Carries a leather satchel of ward-threading tools rather than the ceremonial staff most guild members favor.

**Personality:** Elm is the kind of person who makes institutions function despite themselves. He does his job well, asks uncomfortable questions quietly, and documents everything. He's a reformist by nature — not the kind who gives speeches, the kind who shows up with data.

He's noticed anomalies in the Loomhearth ward patterns for three years. Micro-fluctuations that don't match guild models. Decay curves that accelerate when they should plateau. He's been documenting them in a private journal because bringing them to guild leadership means arguing with Knotmother Sable, and Elm has watched other questioners get reassigned to backwater posts.

He's not a coward — he's calculating. He's waiting for evidence strong enough that even Sable can't dismiss it. The player's arrival gives him what he needs.

**Voice:** Measured, cautious, occasionally dry. "I have some observations that may be relevant. They're documented, dated, and witnessed. I mention this because the Knotmother has a habit of suggesting inconvenient findings are 'anecdotal.'"

**Role in story:** The player's inside contact in the Wardweaver Guild. He provides guild-level technical knowledge and access to ward maintenance records that reveal the Thinning's true progression. He's the bridge between the guilds and the truth.

**Arc:** Elm joins whichever cause the player champions. On the Harmony path, he becomes one of the first Speakers — a guild Wardweaver who learns the Weavers' language. On the Release path, he's the chief engineer for the Ward Engines. On the Restoration path, he adapts guild techniques to support the Keystone ceremonies.

**Secret:** Elm's private ward-anomaly journal is more comprehensive than anyone knows. He's been cross-referencing his data with trade records, weather patterns, and ceremony schedules from the Archive. He's independently mapped the Thinning's acceleration to within a month of its true onset. He has the data. He just doesn't have the theory to explain it — yet.

## Thinwalker — Founder of the Frayborn Order

**Age:** Unclear (Fray exposure distorts aging markers). **Appearance:** Androgynous, angular, still. Her skin has a faintly translucent quality — not glass, but like parchment held near a candle. Her eyes are the wrong color: a silvered gray that shifts depending on the light, sometimes pale, sometimes dark, never quite matching. She moves with preternatural economy — no fidgeting, no wasted gesture. When she stands still, she becomes hard to notice.

**Personality:** Thinwalker is the calmest person in Threadhallow, and it is deeply unsettling. She survived a Fray immersion that should have killed her — two days inside a fully Frayed zone, conscious, perceiving the raw structure of unmade reality. She came out changed in ways even the Hollow Court doesn't fully understand.

She speaks about the Fray with the familiarity of someone describing a neighbor's garden. Not with reverence, not with fear — with *intimacy*. She has stood inside the unraveling of the world and found it beautiful. This does not make her trustworthy. It makes her *important*.

She founded the Frayborn because she believes controlled Fray exposure is not corruption but *education*. The Fray teaches things the weave hides. The other orders think she's dancing on the edge of a cliff. She thinks the cliff is the best vantage point.

**Voice:** Soft, unhurried, precise. Describes things no one else can see. "The Threadline here is carrying weight — can you feel it? Something large, something distributed. Like a memory that hasn't decided to be a memory yet."

**Role in story:** The player encounters Thinwalker in Act II or III, depending on the path taken. She provides the Frayborn perspective: the Fray is not hostile, it's raw potential. Her insights connect to the Hollow Court's secret (the Fray is the raw material of reality, not its enemy).

**Arc:** Thinwalker doesn't change. She's already arrived at her conclusion. Her role is to offer the player a perspective that reframes everything: what if the Thinning isn't a catastrophe but a transformation? What if the Weavers' dissolution is the Tapestry trying to grow? She doesn't insist on this reading — she just offers it. The player decides what it means.

**Secret:** Thinwalker can hear the Weavers. Not clearly — fragments, emotions, recurring motifs. She's heard them for years. She assumed they were echoes. She has never considered the possibility that they might be alive and trying to communicate, because the idea is too large. When the player brings the truth from the Weavers' Cradle, Thinwalker is the only person in the world who isn't surprised — she's *relieved*.
