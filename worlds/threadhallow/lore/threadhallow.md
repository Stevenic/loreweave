# Threadhallow — World Bible

*The base world for LoreWeave. Folk horror meets high fantasy — a world held together by ancient rituals and woven enchantments, where the wilds remember what civilization forgot.*

---

## 1. The Name

**Threadhallow** — from *thread* (the woven enchantments that bind reality) and *hallow* (sacred, consecrated ground). The world itself is hallowed ground, stitched into existence by the first Weavers. The name carries both reverence and warning: what was hallowed can be unhallowed.

---

## 2. The Core Premise

Reality in Threadhallow is not natural — it is **woven**. The world exists because ancient beings called the **Weavers** sang it into a vast, living tapestry. Mountains are knots of dense thread. Rivers are channels where the weave runs thin. Forests grow where threads tangle and fray into organic complexity.

The Weavers are gone. No one knows why. They left behind the **Loom** — the metaphysical framework that holds the tapestry together — and the **Threadlines**, visible only to those with the Sight, which run through the land like ley lines of woven power.

For centuries, mortal civilizations learned to maintain the weave through **ritual**. Village elders sing the Binding Songs at solstice. Wardweavers stitch protective patterns into settlement boundaries. Threadwalkers patrol the wilds, mending tears before they spread. These traditions are the immune system of reality itself.

But the rituals are failing. The songs are being forgotten. And in the places where the weave frays, **the Unraveling** seeps in — a creeping dissolution where the rules of the world stop applying. Physics bends. Time loops. Creatures from the spaces *between* threads slip through.

---

## 3. Major Themes

### 3.1 Memory vs. Forgetting

The world persists because people *remember* how to maintain it. Rituals, songs, stories, and traditions are not just culture — they are structural. When a village forgets the Binding Song, the ward around it weakens. When the last person who knew a region's Thread-name dies, that region becomes vulnerable to Unraveling.

**Gameplay implication:** The player's actions — exploring, talking to NPCs, completing quests — can recover lost knowledge. Finding an old Wardweaver's journal doesn't just give lore; it can literally restabilize a region.

### 3.2 The Old vs. The New

Some factions want to restore the ancient ways — rebuild the Weavers' original patterns, revive every forgotten song. Others believe the old ways are gone and mortals must forge new methods of binding reality. Neither side is wrong. The old rituals are proven but incomplete (the Weavers left gaps). New methods are innovative but untested and sometimes dangerous.

**Gameplay implication:** Quest outcomes often involve choosing between preservation and innovation. Neither is coded as "good" or "evil."

### 3.3 Folk Horror — The Familiar Made Wrong

Threadhallow is not grimdark. Villages have festivals, children play, traders gossip. But there is always something *slightly off* at the edges. A scarecrow that wasn't there yesterday. A lullaby with one wrong note that no one else notices. Flowers blooming in a pattern that looks like a face.

The horror is atmospheric, rooted in folklore logic: don't step outside the ward-circle after dark. Don't answer if something in the forest calls your name. Never unravel a Weaver's knot, even if it looks like it's choking the tree.

**Gameplay implication:** The world is cozy and inviting in settled areas, unsettling in the wilds, and genuinely dangerous where the weave has frayed. Tone shifts with distance from civilization.

### 3.4 Interconnection

Everything is connected — literally, through the weave. Cutting a Threadline in one region can cause a famine three regions away. Healing a corrupt Pattern might calm storms on the opposite coast. The player gradually learns that their actions have far-reaching consequences through the tapestry.

**Gameplay implication:** Event-sourced world state. Actions propagate through the weave. The game's recovery/healing rules map directly to the narrative of the weave repairing itself.

### 3.5 Discovery Over Exposition

The world's history is not delivered in lore dumps. It's embedded in the environment: the Pattern stitched into a ruin's foundation, the half-remembered verse an innkeeper hums, the strange alignment of standing stones that only makes sense after you've visited three other sites. The player pieces together what happened to the Weavers through exploration, not textbooks.

## 4. Cosmology

### 4.1 The Three Layers

| Layer | Name | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **The Tapestry** | Threadhallow itself | The woven world — everything players see, touch, and explore |
| **The Loom** | (unseen) | The metaphysical framework that holds the Tapestry together. Cannot be visited but can be perceived by Threadwalkers. Damage to the Loom manifests as Unraveling in the Tapestry |
| **The Fray** | (the between-space) | What exists outside the woven world — raw, unstructured potential. Not a place, not void. Things that come from the Fray are not evil, just *unformed* and wrong by the Tapestry's rules |

### 4.2 The Weavers

The beings who created Threadhallow. Not gods — closer to cosmic artisans. They wove reality from raw material drawn out of the Fray, imposing pattern and structure on formlessness.

**What's known:**
- They worked collectively — no single Weaver could sustain the Tapestry alone
- They communicated through song (the Binding Songs are degraded echoes)
- They left physical traces: Loom Stones (standing stones that anchor Threadlines), Weaver's Marks (patterns carved into ancient architecture), and the Threadlines themselves
- They vanished approximately 1,200 years ago in an event called **the Silence** — mid-song, mid-work, as if they all stopped at once

**What's unknown (and drives the overarching mystery):**
- Why they left
- Whether they're dead, sleeping, trapped, or simply *elsewhere*
- Whether they intended mortals to inherit the maintenance of the weave
- What the Tapestry was *for* — was Threadhallow a home, a prison, a work of art, or something else?

### 4.3 Magic System — Threadcraft

All magic in Threadhallow is a form of weaving. Practitioners manipulate the threads of reality, which maps onto the D&D 5e spell system:

| Threadcraft Tradition | D&D Analogue | Method |
|----------------------|--------------|--------|
| **Wardweaving** | Abjuration / Protection | Stitching protective patterns into locations or objects |
| **Songbinding** | Enchantment / Bardic | Using the remnants of Weaver-songs to influence minds and emotions |
| **Patternreading** | Divination | Interpreting the weave to see connections, futures, hidden truths |
| **Threadwalking** | Transmutation / Movement | Following Threadlines to travel, phase through the weave, or reshape matter |
| **Unweaving** | Evocation / Destruction | Pulling threads apart to release energy — powerful but dangerous |
| **Fraytouch** | Forbidden | Drawing power from the Fray itself. Effective but corrupts the local weave |

## 5. The State of the World — "Now"

### 5.1 The Current Era: The Thinning

The game takes place during **the Thinning** — a period where the weave is degrading faster than mortal traditions can maintain it. This is not a sudden apocalypse but a slow erosion:

- Wardweaver lineages dying out without passing on knowledge
- Trade and migration disrupting local ritual traditions
- The Unraveling appearing in more places, more often, and lasting longer
- Creatures from the Fray becoming bolder — not invasions, but *seepage*

The world is not ending tomorrow. But it is *fraying*, and the rate is accelerating.

### 5.2 Civilization

Settlement exists on a spectrum of stability:

| Settlement Type | Weave Stability | Character |
|----------------|----------------|-----------|
| **Major cities** | Strong — maintained by Wardweaver guilds | Prosperous, sometimes complacent. Ward maintenance is infrastructure, like roads or sewers |
| **Towns** | Moderate — a resident Wardweaver or visiting Threadwalker | Functional but aware of the wilds. Festivals have real ritual purpose |
| **Villages** | Fragile — dependent on oral traditions | Intimate, superstitious, and the most interesting narratively. One forgotten verse away from vulnerability |
| **Outposts** | Minimal — temporary wards only | Frontier settlements: miners, loggers, trappers. Pragmatic and hardy |
| **Ruins** | None — the weave has frayed | Former settlements lost to the Unraveling. Not destroyed — *changed*. Architecture still stands but the rules are wrong inside |

### 5.3 The Wilds

Between settlements, the world is a patchwork of stable and unstable regions:

- **Stable wilds** — The weave is intact. Normal forests, plains, mountains. Dangerous in the D&D sense (wolves, goblins, bandits) but the world makes sense.
- **Thin places** — The weave is weak. Minor anomalies: perpetual fog, trees growing in spirals, sounds that shouldn't carry. Experienced Threadwalkers can mend these.
- **Frayed zones** — The weave is torn. Reality glitches: gravity shifts, time stutters, spaces loop back on themselves. Hostile Fray-touched creatures. Dangerous but contain valuable Weaver artifacts.
- **Unraveled zones** — The weave is gone. Not nothingness — worse. The raw Fray leaks through. These areas are off-limits to all but the most powerful or desperate. They grow slowly if not contained.

## 6. Factions (Overview)

Detailed faction profiles will be in `world/factions/`. High-level overview:

### 6.1 The Threadwalker Orders

Wandering maintainers of the weave. Think rangers crossed with monks. Multiple orders with different philosophies:
- **The Mending Path** — conservative, focused on restoration of original patterns
- **The New Stitch** — progressive, developing novel binding techniques
- **The Frayborn** — extremists who believe controlled exposure to the Fray strengthens the weave (controversial, possibly right)

### 6.2 The Wardweaver Guilds

Urban-based, guild-structured practitioners who maintain city wards. Politically powerful, sometimes corrupt. Control access to Threadcraft education.

### 6.3 The Rememberers

Scholars, lorekeepers, and archivists dedicated to recovering lost Weaver knowledge. Not practitioners — they collect, translate, and preserve. Often found in ruins.

### 6.4 The Hollow Court

A loose network of Fray-touched individuals who have survived contact with the Unraveling and gained strange abilities. Distrusted by everyone. Are they corrupted, or evolved?

### 6.5 The Common Folk

Not a faction, but a force. Villages maintain their own traditions independent of the guilds and orders. Their folk rituals may be crude but are often older and more potent than the guilds admit.

## 7. The Player's Role

The player begins as an outsider arriving at a small settlement on the boundary between stable and thinning territory. They are not a chosen one. They are not prophesied. They are someone who showed up at a bad time and proved useful.

**Starting hooks:**
- The village's Wardweaver has just died, and nobody knows the Binding Song
- Strange things are happening in the nearby forest — not dangerous yet, but wrong
- A Threadwalker passed through recently and never came back

The player's arc is one of **growing understanding**: from helping a single village, to understanding regional connections, to grasping the scope of the Thinning, to potentially uncovering what happened to the Weavers.

## 8. Tone Guide

### Do

- Ground the fantastical in sensory detail — the ward-circle smells of burnt lavender and old copper
- Let the world be beautiful and inviting before it becomes unsettling
- Make NPCs feel like real people with mundane concerns alongside existential ones
- Use folklore logic — rules that are consistent but unexplained, like fairy tales
- Let horror emerge from *wrongness*, not violence — a bird singing backward, a shadow facing the wrong way

### Don't

- Edgelord grimdark — the world has hope, warmth, and humor
- Explain everything — the mystery of the Weavers should never be fully resolved
- Make any faction purely evil — even the Hollow Court has sympathetic members
- Use real-world cultural stereotypes as shorthand for fantasy cultures
- Deliver lore in exposition dumps — embed it in the environment and NPC dialogue

## 10. The Calendar — The Wheel of Binding

Threadhallow follows a **four-season calendar** with eight significant dates — four solstices/equinoxes and four cross-quarter days. These are not just cultural holidays; they are **mechanically significant ward-renewal ceremonies**. Miss one, and the local weave weakens measurably.

### 10.1 The Eight Bindings

| Date | Name | Season Marker | Ritual Purpose |
|------|------|--------------|----------------|
| **Midwinter Solstice** | The Deep Stitch | Winter peak | Reinforcement of foundation wards — the longest night requires the strongest binding |
| **Late Winter Cross** | Thaw Singing | Winter → Spring | Waking the dormant Threadlines — communities sing through the night to coax life back |
| **Spring Equinox** | The Greening Knot | Spring midpoint | Renewal of growth-wards on farmland and orchards. Wardweavers re-stitch fertility patterns |
| **Late Spring Cross** | Bright Mending | Spring → Summer | Repair season — Threadwalkers return to settlements to report and mend winter damage |
| **Midsummer Solstice** | The High Weave | Summer peak | The strongest day. New wards are woven, apprentices are tested, ward-circles are expanded |
| **Late Summer Cross** | The Thinning Watch | Summer → Autumn | Vigilance ceremony — communities walk their ward boundaries, checking for fraying |
| **Autumn Equinox** | The Gathering Thread | Autumn midpoint | Harvest and consolidation. Wards are tightened inward as the world prepares to contract |
| **Late Autumn Cross** | The Hollowing | Autumn → Winter | The most dangerous night. The Fray presses closest. Communities huddle inside ward-circles. Doors are stitched shut with thread |

### 10.2 Mechanical Significance

- **Ward strength** is measured in 8 increments per year, decaying between ceremonies. A settlement that performs all 8 Bindings maintains full ward strength. Each missed ceremony reduces stability by ~12%.
- **The Hollowing** (late autumn) is the most critical — the Fray is most active when the world is "shedding" for winter. Missing the Hollowing is worse than missing any other Binding.
- **Player opportunity**: Arriving at a village before a Binding ceremony and helping them perform it (recovering lost verses, defending against disruption) is a natural quest hook.
- **Time-of-year** affects gameplay: exploration is safest in midsummer (wards at peak, long days), most dangerous in late autumn and midwinter (wards decaying, Fray active).

### 10.3 Months

Twelve months, each named for what the weave does during that period:

| # | Month Name | Season | Character |
|---|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | **Stillthread** | Early Winter | The weave contracts. Quiet, cold, reflective |
| 2 | **Deepknot** | Mid Winter | Deepest binding. The world holds its breath |
| 3 | **Thawsong** | Late Winter | First stirring. Faint hum in the Threadlines |
| 4 | **Greenweave** | Early Spring | Explosive growth. The weave stretches |
| 5 | **Brightloom** | Mid Spring | Peak vitality. Threadlines sing visibly |
| 6 | **Longstitch** | Late Spring | Long days of mending. Longest light |
| 7 | **Highthread** | Early Summer | Peak stability. The weave is tightest |
| 8 | **Goldenweft** | Mid Summer | Warmth and abundance. Festivals, trade |
| 9 | **Wanespindle** | Late Summer | First hints of loosening. Watchful |
| 10 | **Fraywatch** | Early Autumn | The Thinning accelerates. Tension rises |
| 11 | **Harvestknot** | Mid Autumn | Gathering in — thread, food, knowledge |
| 12 | **Hollowdark** | Late Autumn | The Fray at its strongest. Danger season |

## 11. Naming Conventions

Threadhallow names draw from **Anglo-Celtic folk roots** — earthy, grounded, and slightly archaic. Names should feel like they belong in a village that's been there for four hundred years.

### 11.1 Settlement Names

Compound structure: **[landscape/material] + [feature/suffix]**

| Pattern | Examples |
|---------|---------|
| **Nature + structure** | Thornwick, Bracken Fell, Ashenmoor, Hazelgate |
| **Material + place** | Stonehaven, Coppervale, Ironbend, Timbercross |
| **Water + feature** | Millhaven, Brookhollow, Reedmere, Stillwater |
| **Old + sacred** | Eldergrove, Hallowfield, Bindstone, Wardreach |
| **Thread-specific** | Loomhearth, Stitchford, Weftbridge, Threadfall |

Settlements near Frayed zones often have **warning names**: Woundmere, Hollowreach, Frayend, Silentwick.

### 11.2 Personal Names

Anglo-Celtic-inspired given names with occasional woven/thread motifs:

- **Common folk**: Rowan, Hazel, Bryn, Aldric, Maren, Colm, Eira, Tam, Sorrel, Wynn
- **Wardweavers**: Often take a thread-name upon joining a guild — Knotmother Sable, Stitchwright Elm, Wardkeeper Ashe
- **Threadwalkers**: Use path-names — Farweft, Longstitch, Thinwalker, Fraywatch
- **Rememberers**: Take the name of a text they've sworn to preserve — Archivist of the Third Verse, Keeper of Hollow Songs

### 11.3 Creature & Place Names

Folklore logic — descriptive, slightly ominous:
- **Fray creatures**: The Unspun, Looselings, Frayed Ones, Thread-eaters, Hollow Walkers
- **Landmarks**: The Knotstone, Old Loom Hill, The Singing Cairn, Weaver's Rest, The Torn Meadow
- **Phenomena**: Threadfall (a rain of loose threads from a tearing sky), Hollow Wind (breeze from a Frayed zone), Bright Hum (Threadlines resonating)

## 12. The Weavers' Fate — The Main Quest

The central mystery of Threadhallow is **discoverable through gameplay** — not handed to the player, but pieced together through exploration, artifact recovery, and NPC dialogue across the game's main quest arc.

### 12.1 The Truth (Spoilers)

The Weavers did not die, flee, or ascend. They **wove themselves into the Tapestry**.

The Tapestry was failing — a fundamental flaw in its structure meant it couldn't sustain itself indefinitely. Rather than let their creation unravel, the Weavers sacrificed their individual consciousness and became part of the weave itself. The Threadlines are not just remnants of their work — they *are* the Weavers, distributed throughout reality.

This is why the weave has self-healing properties. This is why Threadlines hum. This is why the Binding Songs work — they're not invoking absent creators, they're *harmonizing with them*.

The Thinning is happening because the Weavers-as-weave are slowly losing coherence. Consciousness, even distributed, erodes over 1,200 years. The songs and rituals that mortals perform don't just maintain the weave — they *remind* the Weavers who they are.

### 12.2 Discovery Arc (5 stages)

| Stage | The Player Learns | How |
|-------|-------------------|-----|
| 1. **Local** | The Weavers existed and vanished | Village lore, ruins, Loom Stones |
| 2. **Regional** | The Silence was deliberate, not sudden | Rememberer archives, contradictions in the record |
| 3. **Continental** | The Weavers were trying to fix the Tapestry, not flee it | Major Threadwalker order quest chains |
| 4. **Cosmological** | The Threadlines respond to direct address — they're not inert | Artifact interactions, Fray-edge encounters |
| 5. **Truth** | The Weavers are the weave. The Binding Songs are conversations | Ancient Loom Stone that still carries a Weaver's fragmented voice |

### 12.3 Player Choice at Endgame

Once the truth is known, the player faces a choice:
- **Restore** — Find a way to re-cohere the Weavers' consciousness. Risks: they might reclaim the Tapestry and leave mortals as passengers in someone else's world again
- **Release** — Let the Weavers fade completely. Mortals inherit the weave fully, but lose its self-healing properties. The world becomes fragile but truly *theirs*
- **Harmonize** — Teach mortal civilizations the true nature of the Binding Songs so they can sustain the Weavers-as-weave indefinitely. A partnership, not a rescue. Hardest path, best outcome, requires completing the most content

## 13. The Scale of the Unraveling

The Thinning is a **permanent, accelerating condition** — not a doomsday clock.

### 13.1 Why Not a Timer?

A ticking clock conflicts with open-world exploration. Players should feel free to wander, build, experiment, and roleplay without existential pressure. The Thinning provides *stakes* without *urgency*:

- The world is getting worse, but not on a schedule
- Specific locations can deteriorate or stabilize based on player action (and inaction)
- The overall trend is downward, but local victories are meaningful and persistent

### 13.2 How the Thinning Manifests

The Thinning is not uniform — it's patchy, organic, and responsive to world state:

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Missed Binding ceremonies** | Settlement ward strength decays 12% per missed ceremony |
| **Wardweaver death without successor** | Region enters accelerated decay over 2-4 game months |
| **Player restores a Loom Stone** | Stabilizes a wide radius — reverses decay for several chunks |
| **Fray creature incursion** | Local weave damage. Self-heals slowly if small, persists if large |
| **Major quest completion** | Can permanently stabilize a region or even push back the Fray |

### 13.3 The World Adapts

The Thinning is not just entropy — the world responds:

- **New traditions emerge** — villages invent crude but effective ward rituals
- **Fray ecology develops** — some creatures from the Fray become naturalized, neither hostile nor friendly
- **Ruins become resources** — old Weaver infrastructure is mined for fragments of binding power
- **The Hollow Court grows** — more people survive Fray contact, creating a new faction dynamic

The world is not dying — it is *changing*. Whether that change is degradation or evolution depends on the player's perspective and choices.

## 14. The Pantheon — The Loom-Born

The Weavers are **not gods**. Threadhallow has a separate divine layer: the **Loom-Born**, beings who emerged from the Tapestry after it was woven. They are powerful but *part of the weave*, not its creators.

### 14.1 Theological Structure

- The Weavers created the Tapestry
- The Tapestry, once complex enough, spontaneously generated consciousness within itself — the Loom-Born
- The Loom-Born are *patterns* given agency — each embodies an aspect of the woven world
- They are genuinely divine (clerics channel their power, paladins swear oaths to them), but they are also subject to the Thinning. If the weave fails, they fail too

This creates a profound tension: **the gods need mortal help**. They can't fix the Thinning themselves because they are part of the thing that's thinning.

### 14.2 The Six Loom-Born

| Loom-Born | Domain | Aspect | D&D Domains |
|-----------|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Kael Thornmantle** | The Wild Pattern | Nature, growth, the green tangle | Nature, Life, Tempest |
| **Seren Stillwater** | The Measured Thread | Knowledge, memory, the archive | Knowledge, Order, Arcana |
| **Voss Ironweft** | The Binding Knot | Protection, endurance, craft | Forge, War, Protection |
| **Asha Brightloom** | The Shining Thread | Light, joy, community, hearth | Light, Life, Peace |
| **Morrith Hollowdark** | The Frayed Edge | Death, mystery, the liminal | Grave, Twilight, Death |
| **Tael Quickstitch** | The Loose Thread | Change, trickery, freedom, chaos | Trickery, Tempest, Nature |

### 14.3 Worship

- Not organized religion in the church sense — more like **acknowledgment of the weave's aspects**
- Prayers are woven gestures (knotting a thread, unraveling a cord, tracing a pattern)
- Temples are rare — the weave itself is the temple. Worship happens at Loom Stones, ward-circles, and natural Pattern formations
- Clerics are individuals who resonate so deeply with a Loom-Born's pattern that they can channel its thread directly
- The Loom-Born do not speak in words — they communicate through *pattern recognition*: a cleric's prayer is answered when the desired pattern appears in the world around them

### 14.4 The Loom-Born and the Main Quest

As the player discovers the Weavers' true fate (§12), the Loom-Born's nature takes on new significance:
- They are the Tapestry becoming aware of itself — echoes of the Weavers' patterns, but not the Weavers themselves
- They know *something* is wrong (the Thinning) but can't perceive the Weavers directly — like a painting sensing the painter's absence
- Late-game content involves the Loom-Born grappling with the revelation that they are children of a sacrifice they can't remember
